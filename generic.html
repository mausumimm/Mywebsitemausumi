October 22, 2022

Forecasting Energy Gap in Ireland
Business Forecasting (Masters)

The project, which started in August 2020, intends to estimate energy supply and demand trends in Ireland over one and four years. We chose the Holt-Winters model for forecasting after extensive model testing because of its unrivalled accuracy. As we looked deeper into future energy demand, we used the seasonal-naive model to estimate the energy gap between supply and consumption. For the year 2024, the projections showed an energy output of around 2950 GWh (monthly peak value) versus a demand of around 3900 GWh (monthly peak value). These findings highlight remarkable consistency in energy production patterns, including renewable sources, from 2020 to 2024. Consumption, on the other hand, is expected to rise by 3% throughout this time period.A approach to bridging the energy gap appears to be the optimal deployment of renewable energy power plants. This strategy minimises not just reliance on nonrenewable resources, but also energy and operational expenses. Wind, solar, and other renewable energy sources are viable options. A single wind turbine, for example, can generate up to 6 GWh per year on average. Similarly, solar panels can create up to 0.0026 GWh, which is enough to meet a portion of the 0.0045 GWh energy requirement per residence.

Reasons for Attrition at IBM
Data Mining (Masters)

The purpose of this project was to identify the factors that affect an employee’s attrition. Given that Logistic Regression had the highest accuracy, lowest misclassification, good sensitivity, and was classified as the best predictor for attrition within IBM, we advise IBM to pay close attention to two factors that contribute significantly to attrition in the company. Here we are using intercept to compare different variables to select the ones that highly impact the attrition rate, as logistic regression analysis estimates the log odds of an event. The model suggests that OverTime is the most significant influencer of attrition rate. Using logistic regression, we observed that employees who spent extra hours at work had the highest likelihood of leaving the company.
